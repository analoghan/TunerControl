DI Fuel Pressure Deep Dive — Help

This tool provides detailed analysis of direct injection fuel rail pressure behavior, tracking accuracy, transient response, and operating condition correlation to diagnose HPFP and fuel system issues.

Quick Start

  1. Load a MoTeC log file (.csv or .ld) containing fuel pressure, fuel pressure aim, and engine speed channels.
  2. Click Analyze.
  3. Review the pressure error statistics, transient response events, and correlation charts.
  4. Check diagnostics for overshoot/undershoot warnings and HPFP capacity issues.

Input Requirements

Required Channels

  • Fuel Pressure Direct Bank 1 — actual rail pressure (dMPa in MoTeC, displayed as bar)
  • Fuel Pressure Direct Bank 1 Aim — ECU target rail pressure
  • Engine Speed — RPM for correlation analysis

Optional Channels

  • Fuel Pressure Direct Bank 1 Control — pump control duty/output signal. Shows how hard the ECU is driving the HPFP.
  • Fuel Injector Primary Duty Cycle — injector duty. Correlates pressure error with fuel demand.
  • Fuel Flow — instantaneous fuel flow rate. Correlates pressure drops with high flow demand.
  • Fuel Volume — cumulative fuel consumed.
  • Throttle Pedal — driver demand for transient context.
Note: All three required channels must be present. Without fuel pressure, aim, and RPM, the tool cannot perform its analysis.

Key Metrics

Error Statistics

The tool computes the pressure error as actual - aim for every sample:

MetricMeaningHealthy Range
Mean ErrorAverage offset from aim across the log±0.5 bar
Mean Absolute ErrorAverage magnitude of deviation (ignores direction)< 2 bar
Std Dev ErrorVariability of pressure tracking< 3 bar
Max OvershootWorst case above aim< 10 bar
Max UndershootWorst case below aim> -10 bar
% Within ToleranceSamples within ±2 bar of aim> 95%
% OvershootSamples more than 2 bar above aim< 5%
% UndershootSamples more than 2 bar below aim< 5%
Tolerance band: The tool uses ±2 bar as the acceptable tolerance. A well-functioning HPFP system should keep pressure within this band >95% of the time during steady-state operation. Transient excursions outside this band are normal during rapid aim changes.

Transient Detection

The tool automatically detects transient events — moments where the fuel pressure aim changes rapidly (more than 20 bar within 0.5 seconds). For each transient:

  • Direction — whether aim stepped up or down
  • Aim change magnitude — how large the step was (bar)
  • Max error during transient — worst overshoot or undershoot during the response
  • Settle time — how long until pressure returns within ±2 bar of the new aim

Example transient event:

Direction: Up | Aim change: +45 bar | Max error: +8 bar overshoot | Settle time: 0.35s

What Good Transient Response Looks Like

ParameterGoodMarginalPoor
Settle time (step up)< 0.3s0.3–0.8s> 0.8s
Settle time (step down)< 0.5s0.5–1.0s> 1.0s
Overshoot on step up< 5 bar5–15 bar> 15 bar
Undershoot on step down< 5 bar5–15 bar> 15 bar
Step-down is slower: Pressure reduction relies on fuel being consumed by the injectors (there's no pressure relief valve in most DI systems). Step-down settle time is naturally longer than step-up, especially at low RPM where injector events are less frequent.

Charts

Pressure vs Aim Timeline

Full-width time series showing actual rail pressure and aim overlaid. The ±2 bar tolerance band is shaded around the aim line. Deviations outside this band are immediately visible. Use zoom (scroll) and pan (drag) to inspect specific events.

Pressure Error Timeline

The error signal (actual - aim) plotted over time with the ±2 bar tolerance band. Positive = overshoot, negative = undershoot. This is the primary diagnostic chart — patterns in the error signal reveal system behavior.

Error vs RPM

Scatter plot correlating pressure error with engine speed. Look for:

  • Undershoot increasing with RPM — HPFP capacity limit (can't pump fast enough at high RPM)
  • Overshoot at low RPM — control gain too aggressive at low speeds
  • Consistent error across all RPM — systematic offset (sensor calibration or aim table issue)

Error vs Injector Duty Cycle

Correlates pressure error with fuel demand. If undershoot increases above 70% duty cycle, the pump is reaching its flow capacity. This is the clearest indicator of HPFP limitation on modified engines.

Error vs Fuel Flow

Similar to duty cycle correlation but uses actual fuel flow rate. Useful for identifying the exact flow rate where the pump starts falling behind.

Transient Response Overlay

All detected transient events time-aligned to t=0 (the moment aim changes) and overlaid. Shows the pressure error response pattern. Consistent traces indicate predictable system behavior; scattered traces suggest inconsistent pump response.

Error Distribution (Histogram)

Distribution of pressure error values across the entire log. A healthy system shows a tight, symmetric distribution centered near 0. A skewed distribution indicates systematic over- or under-pressure. A wide distribution indicates poor control stability.

Diagnostics

The tool generates diagnostic messages based on the analysis:

  • "Pressure tracks aim well" — >95% within tolerance. System is healthy.
  • "Significant undershoot detected" — >10% of samples below aim by more than 2 bar. Pump may be struggling.
  • "Significant overshoot detected" — >10% of samples above aim by more than 2 bar. Control gain may be too aggressive.
  • "Worst undershoot/overshoot" — reports the extreme values and at what RPM they occurred.
  • "Transient events detected" — reports count and average settle time.
  • "High duty cycle correlation" — undershoot increases above 70% injector duty, indicating HPFP capacity limit.

GM LT4 HPFP System

System Architecture

ComponentSpecification
Low-pressure pumpIn-tank electric, supplies 4–6 bar to HPFP inlet
High-pressure pumpCam-driven single-piston, 3-lobe cam on exhaust camshaft
Maximum pressure~200 bar (20 MPa)
Fuel railCommon rail feeding all 8 injectors
Pressure sensorMounted on fuel rail, 0–200 bar range
Control methodSolenoid-controlled inlet metering valve on HPFP
Pump drive3 pumping events per camshaft revolution (6 per crank revolution)

How the HPFP Control Works

The M1 ECU controls rail pressure by modulating the HPFP inlet metering valve:

  1. The ECU determines the target pressure from the Fuel Pressure Aim table (indexed by RPM and load)
  2. A closed-loop controller compares actual vs aim and adjusts the metering valve duty
  3. Higher duty = more fuel pumped per stroke = pressure rises
  4. Lower duty = less fuel pumped = pressure falls (consumed by injectors)
The "Fuel Pressure Direct Bank 1 Control" channel shows this control output. Values near 100% mean the pump is working at maximum capacity. If you see sustained 100% control with pressure still below aim, the pump has reached its physical limit.

Pressure Aim Table Strategy

The fuel pressure aim table in M1 Tune typically follows this pattern:

ConditionTypical Aim (bar)Rationale
Idle / cranking50–80Low demand, keep PW above minimum for stability
Light cruise80–120Moderate demand, balance efficiency and response
Part throttle120–160Increasing demand, prepare for potential tip-in
Full load / boost160–200Maximum fuel delivery capacity needed
Overrun / decel50–80No fuel demand, reduce pump load
Modified engines: If you've increased injector size or boost levels, you may need higher pressure aims to maintain adequate fuel delivery. However, higher pressure means shorter pulse widths — watch for XDI instability at idle if pressure aim is too high at low load.

Failure Modes & Diagnosis

HPFP Capacity Limitation

Symptoms in this tool:

  • Undershoot increases with RPM (Error vs RPM chart shows downward trend at high RPM)
  • Undershoot increases with duty cycle (Error vs Duty chart shows negative correlation above 70%)
  • Control channel pegged at or near 100% during undershoot events
  • Diagnostic: "High duty cycle correlation: undershoot increases above 70% duty"

Causes: Stock pump capacity exceeded (modified engine), cam lobe wear, inlet restriction, low-pressure supply insufficient.

Solutions: Upgraded HPFP cam lobe, higher-flow low-pressure pump, reduced pressure aim at high RPM (trade-off: longer PW needed), or aftermarket HPFP.

Control Instability (Oscillation)

Symptoms:

  • High std dev error during steady-state operation
  • Error timeline shows regular oscillation pattern
  • Histogram is wide and possibly bimodal

Causes: Control gains too aggressive, sensor noise, mechanical resonance in fuel system.

Solutions: Reduce P gain in fuel pressure controller, add filtering to pressure sensor input, check for air in fuel lines.

Slow Transient Response

Symptoms:

  • Settle times consistently > 0.5s for step-up events
  • Transient overlay shows slow, sluggish response curves
  • Large undershoot during tip-in (pressure can't rise fast enough)

Causes: Control gains too conservative, HPFP inlet valve response slow, low-pressure supply lag.

Solutions: Increase P gain (carefully — too much causes oscillation), verify low-pressure pump maintains supply during transients, check for fuel filter restriction.

Systematic Offset

Symptoms:

  • Mean error consistently positive or negative (not centered at 0)
  • Error vs RPM shows flat offset across all speeds
  • Histogram centered away from 0

Causes: Pressure sensor calibration drift, aim table offset, control integral term saturated.

Solutions: Verify sensor calibration with known reference, check for integral wind-up limits in controller, recalibrate sensor offset in M1 Tune.

Tuning Workflow

  1. Baseline log: Drive through full operating range including idle, cruise, part-throttle, WOT, and tip-in/tip-out transients. Log at high sample rate (200+ Hz for fuel pressure channels).
  2. Analyze: Load the log and review the summary. Check % within tolerance and identify any systematic patterns.
  3. Check steady-state: If % within tolerance is below 95%, look at the Error vs RPM chart to identify which RPM ranges have the worst tracking.
  4. Check transients: Review transient events. If settle times are poor, adjust control gains. If overshoot is excessive, reduce P gain. If response is sluggish, increase P gain.
  5. Check capacity: Look at Error vs Duty Cycle. If undershoot correlates with high duty, the pump is at its limit — either reduce pressure aim or upgrade hardware.
  6. Adjust and re-log: Make targeted changes to pressure aim table or control gains. Re-log and verify improvement.

Typical iteration:

1. Baseline: 87% within tolerance, undershoot at high RPM

2. Error vs Duty shows undershoot above 75% duty → pump capacity issue

3. Reduce pressure aim at high RPM/load from 200 → 180 bar

4. Re-log: 96% within tolerance, no undershoot correlation with duty

5. Verify injector PW is still above 0.8ms at the lower pressure (check with Injector tool)

Safety: Fuel pressure that drops significantly below aim under boost means the engine is running lean. If you see sustained undershoot (>10 bar below aim) at high load, reduce boost or load until the fuel system issue is resolved. Running lean under boost risks catastrophic engine damage.
Pro tip: Use this tool in conjunction with the Injector Characterization tool. The fuel pressure aim table directly affects injector pulse width — lowering pressure aim increases PW (good for idle stability) but reduces maximum fuel delivery capacity (bad for WOT). Finding the right balance requires checking both tools after each change.
